[3 points]
Let $f(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & x < 0 \\ \alpha x^2 & 0 \leq x \leq 2 \\ 0 & 2 < x \end{cases}$ For what value of $\alpha$ is $f(x)$ a valid probability density function?

A probabilty density function must integrate to 1, so we can solve for $\alpha$ by setting the integral of $f(x)$ equal to 1 and solving for $\alpha$:

$$ \begin{align} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) dx &= 1 \\ \int_{-\infty}^{0} 0 dx + \int_{0}^{2} \alpha x^2 dx + \int_{2}^{\infty} 0 dx &= 1 \\ \frac{\alpha}{3} x^3 \Big|_{0}^{2} &= 1 \\ \frac{\alpha}{3} (2^3 - 0^3) &= 1 \\ \frac{8}{3} \alpha &= 1 \\ \alpha &= \frac{3}{8} \end{align} $$

2¶

[3 points] What is the cumulative distribution function (CDF) that corresponds to the following probability distribution function? Please state the value of the CDF for all possible values of $x$.

$f(x) = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{3} & 0 < x < 3 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$

ANSWER

The CDF is the integral of the PDF, so we can solve for the CDF by integrating $f(x)$:

$$ \begin{align} F(x) &= \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(x) dx \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{0} 0 dx + \int_{0}^{x} \frac{1}{3} dx + \int_{x}^{3} 0 dx \\ &= \frac{1}{3} x \Big|_{0}^{x} \\ &= \frac{1}{3} x \end{align} $$

The CDF is: $f(x) = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{3}x & 0 < x < 3 \\ 0 & x \leq 0 \\ 1 & x \geq 3 \end{cases}$

3¶

[6 points] For the probability distribution function for the random variable $X$,

$f(x) = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{3} & 0 < x < 3 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$

what is the (a) expected value and (b) variance of $X$. Show all work.

ANSWER

The expected value of $X$ is the mean of $f(x)$:

$$ \begin{align} E[X] &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} xf(x) dx \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{0} 0 dx + \int_{0}^{3} \frac{1}{3} x dx + \int_{3}^{\infty} 0 dx \\ &= \frac{1}{3} \frac{x^2}{2} \Big|_{0}^{3} \\ &= \frac{1}{3} \frac{3^2}{2} \\ &= \frac{3}{2} \end{align} $$

The variance of $X$:

$$ \begin{align} Var[X] &= E[X^2] - E[X]^2 \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x^2 f(x) dx - \left( \frac{3}{2} \right)^2 \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{0} 0 dx + \int_{0}^{3} \frac{1}{3} x^2 dx + \int_{3}^{\infty} 0 dx - \left( \frac{3}{2} \right)^2 \\ &= \frac{1}{3} \frac{x^3}{3} \Big|_{0}^{3} - \left( \frac{3}{2} \right)^2 \\ &= \frac{1}{3} \frac{3^3}{3} - \left( \frac{3}{2} \right)^2 \\ &= 3 - \frac{9}{4} \\ &= \frac{3}{4} \end{align} $$

[5 points] A common task in machine learning is a change of basis: transforming the representation of our data from one space to another. A prime example of this is through the process of dimensionality reduction as in Principle Components Analysis where we often seek to transform our data from one space (of dimension $n$) to a new space (of dimension $m$) where $m<n$. Assume we have a sample of data of dimension $n=4$ (as shown below) and we want to transform it into a dimension of $m=2$.

$\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \\ x_4 \end{bmatrix}$

ANSWER

a) The dimensions of $\mathbf{A}$ are $2 \times 4$. This is because the number of rows in $\mathbf{A}$ must equal the number of columns in $\mathbf{x}$, and the number of columns in $\mathbf{A}$ must equal the number of rows in the transformed $\mathbf{x}$.

b) The transformation is:

$$ \begin{align} \mathbf{Ax} &= \begin{bmatrix} a_{1,1} & a_{1,2} & a_{1,3} & a_{1,4} \\ a_{2,1} & a_{2,2} & a_{2,3} & a_{2,4} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \\ x_4 \end{bmatrix} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} a_{1,1} x_1 + a_{1,2} x_2 + a_{1,3} x_3 + a_{1,4} x_4 \\ a_{2,1} x_1 + a_{2,2} x_2 + a_{2,3} x_3 + a_{2,4} x_4 \end{bmatrix} \end{align} $$

In machine learning, we iterate over so much data that those loops can lead to significant slow downs if they are not computationally efficient. In Python, vectorizing code and relying on matrix operations with efficient tools like numpy is typically the faster approach. Of course, numpy relies on loops to complete the computation, but this is at a lower level of programming (typically in C), and therefore is much more efficient.
